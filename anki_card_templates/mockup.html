Vocabulary Card (ff_vocab)
bouleverser
VERB • TRANSITIVE • 1ST GROUP
/bul.vɛʁ.se/

Meaning

Provoquer un trouble profond, un grand changement dans quelque chose ou quelqu'un ; renverser, mettre sens dessus dessous

Translation

to overwhelm, to upset deeply, to turn upside down, to disrupt completely

Synonyms

chambouler, renverser, troubler, ébranler, secouer

Antonyms

calmer, apaiser, stabiliser, rassurer

Word Family

bouleversement (n.m.) - upheaval bouleversant (adj.) - overwhelming bouleversé (adj./pp.) - overwhelmed, devastated

Etymology

From Old French "bouleverser" - compound of "boule" (ball) + "verser" (to turn), literally "to roll and turn over"

Memory Hook

Think of a BOWL being REVERSED - everything spills out chaotically, just like when your emotions are bouleversé!

Collocations

bouleverser les plans
to upset the plans
être bouleversé par la nouvelle
to be devastated by the news
bouleverser l'ordre établi
to overturn the established order

Examples

Cette nouvelle a complètement bouleversé ma vie.
This news completely turned my life upside down.
La révolution a bouleversé tout le pays.
The revolution disrupted the entire country.
Elle était bouleversée par la mort de son chat.
She was devastated by the death of her cat.

Phrase Card (ff_meaningblocks)
c'est-à-dire
/sɛ.t‿a.diʁ/

Meaning

Locution servant à introduire une explication, une précision ou une reformulation de ce qui vient d'être dit

Translation

that is to say, in other words, i.e., meaning

Usage Notes

Used to clarify or rephrase a statement. Very common in both spoken and written French. Often abbreviated as "c.-à-d." in writing. Can be followed by "que" when introducing a clause.

Examples

Je viendrai demain, c'est-à-dire mardi.
I'll come tomorrow, that is to say, Tuesday.
C'est-à-dire que je ne suis pas sûr de pouvoir venir.
What I mean is, I'm not sure I can come.
Il est polyglotte, c'est-à-dire qu'il parle plusieurs langues.
He's a polyglot, meaning he speaks several languages.
